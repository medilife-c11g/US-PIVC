Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Post-insertion outcomes of ultrasound-guided versus landmark peripheral intravenous catheters: a systematic review and meta-analysis"** for consideration for publication in *The Ultrasound Journal*.

Ultrasound-guided peripheral intravenous catheter (USG-PIVC) insertion has been widely adopted to improve cannulation success, particularly in patients with difficult intravenous access. Multiple systematic reviews have established its insertion benefits. However, no prior systematic review has specifically evaluated whether USG confers any advantage in **post-insertion** outcomes -- including catheter failure, dwell time, and complications such as infiltration and extravasation.

Our systematic review and meta-analysis of 14 studies (5 RCTs, 9 cohort studies; 78,209 participants) across four databases reveals three key findings of direct relevance to the ultrasound community:

1. **USG does not improve post-insertion catheter outcomes** compared with landmark placement (catheter failure RR 1.23, 95% CI 1.00--1.51; dwell time results heterogeneous and unpoolable, I²=91.9%).

2. **Catheter length, not ultrasound guidance, appears to be the primary determinant** of post-insertion catheter performance. Studies reporting favorable USG outcomes were confounded by the use of systematically longer catheters in the USG group -- a finding with important implications for catheter selection when targeting deeper vessels under ultrasound guidance.

3. **Extravasation may represent a safety concern** with USG-placed PIVCs, particularly during high-pressure contrast injection, likely related to the biomechanical mismatch between standard-length catheters and deeper vessels accessed under ultrasound.

These findings challenge the common assumption that insertion success translates to catheter longevity, and provide actionable guidance for clinicians who use point-of-care ultrasound for vascular access.

We believe this work is well suited for *The Ultrasound Journal* given its focus on clinical applications of point-of-care ultrasound and its readership of clinicians who routinely perform USG-PIVC insertion.
